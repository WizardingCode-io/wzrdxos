# Dream 100 Partnerships

Flexible capability skill. Takes the current growth context and produces a structured
Dream 100 list, a warm-up plan, and a first-10 activation playbook. Adapts depth to
context: a full Dream 100 build vs. a single-category deep-dive vs. a whale-account
outreach plan.

Anchored on: Russell Brunson (Dream 100 — "give your way in" to the 100 dream
partners/channels/whales before asking anything — Personas 799 lines, WizardingCode
vault); Dan Martell (parallel outreach for whale accounts — never rely on a single
relationship; build in parallel so no single "no" collapses the pipeline); Alex
Hormozi (value-first relationship building — give 100 times before you ask once).
All sourced from the WizardingCode vault (Topics — Sales Frameworks, Growth Hacking
Aplicado, Lead Generation & Conversion; Personas — Brunson 799 lines, Martell,
Hormozi 169 sources, Suby 775 lines).

## Steps

### 1. KB-first

`kb_search` and `kb_ask` for:
- Company profile (`wzrdx company`) — offer, ICP, current network, geographic focus.
- Any existing partnership lists, referral partner documents, or Dream 100 drafts.
- Diagnostico-2026-05 — Vendas 25/100, pipeline state, €1M target.
- Dream 100 framework (Brunson) — the three categories: partners, channels, whales.
- Parallel outreach protocol (Martell) — whale-account cadence.
- Growth Hacking Aplicado topic — PT market partnership patterns.
- Deal pipeline snapshot — compute the revenue gap the Dream 100 must help close.

Note what already exists. Do not restart a Dream 100 already in motion — update and
extend it.

### 2. Dream 100 categories — build the master list

The Dream 100 is organised into three categories. Each entry is a relationship to
cultivate, not a cold prospect — the frame is "how do we give first?" not "how do
we sell to them?"

**Category A — Partners (referral ecosystem):**
Companies, agencies, consultants or professionals who serve the same ICP but do not
compete with the current offer. When they recommend wzrdx, both sides win.

Examples for a PT-market AI-agency context:
- Digital agencies without AI capability (they need AI; wzrdx supplies it).
- Strategy consultants who implement recommendations but not technology.
- HR platforms with clients undergoing digital transformation.
- Accounting firms serving growing SMEs (COO/CFO pain = systems gap = wzrdx).
- Business accelerators / incubators serving their cohort companies.

**Category B — Channels (distribution amplifiers):**
People, platforms or media that have concentrated access to the ICP. Being in their
feed, newsletter, podcast or community bypasses cold outreach entirely.

Examples:
- Portuguese business podcasts with SME founder audiences.
- LinkedIn thought leaders in the operations / AI / scale-up space in PT/PT-speaking
  markets.
- Industry newsletters (e.g. tech, scale-up, operations in PT).
- Community organisers (founder groups, mastermind hosts).
- Event organisers (entrepreneurship events, tech conferences, association events).

**Category C — Whales (dream direct accounts):**
The 10-20 single accounts that, if closed, would move the revenue needle significantly.
These are not typical ICP leads — they are aspirational clients whose logos would
anchor the case study library and accelerate credibility.

Examples: the PT/EU company in the ICP vertical that every peer looks up to; the
company whose win would fund 6 months of runway; the anchor client that unlocks a
new vertical.

**List-building protocol:**

For each category, list entries using the following fields:

| # | Name | Company / Channel | Why them (ICP fit / audience / revenue impact) | Current relationship | Warm-up status |
|---|------|-------------------|-------------------------------------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| 1 | ... | ... | ... | None / Acquaintance / Warm | Not started / In progress / Active |

Target: 100 total entries across all three categories. Start with 25-30 in the first
session (the "first 30" are the ones you can populate from memory); identify the
remaining 70 through research.

Research tools for the 70 unknown: LinkedIn filtered search, podcast guest lists,
community member directories, industry event speaker lists, competitor case study
pages (who are their public clients?).

### 3. Prioritise — the first 10

From the master list of 100, select the first 10 to activate. Prioritisation criteria:

| Criterion | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Relationship strength (warmest first) | High | The warm-up timeline is shorter; first results come faster |
| Revenue / distribution impact | High | Whale or channel with largest ICP audience = highest ceiling |
| Reciprocity fit | Medium | Can wzrdx give them something genuinely valuable first? |
| Geographic / market fit | Medium | PT-first before Lusofonia before EU |
| Reachability | Medium | Do you have a path to them (mutual connection, event, content)? |

Document the first-10 selection with the rationale for each. The first 10 are not
necessarily the highest revenue — they are the highest-probability first wins that
build momentum.

### 4. Value-first warm-up plan

The Dream 100 is not outreach — it is a relationship-building system. The rule:
give 100 times before you ask once (Hormozi). Asking too early is the cardinal sin.

For each of the first 10, design a warm-up sequence of value-first touches:

**Value-first touch types (in order of effort and impact):**

| Type | Description | Effort | Visibility |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Public amplification | Share, comment, quote or mention their content publicly | Low | High — they see it |
| Useful introduction | Connect them with someone who is genuinely useful to them | Low | Medium |
| Resource gift | Send a specific resource (framework, article, tool) that solves their current problem | Low | Low |
| Collaboration invitation | Co-author a post, appear on each other's platforms | Medium | High |
| Custom research / mini-audit | Produce something uniquely valuable for their business and send it unprompted | High | High — very memorable |
| Event connection | Attend the same event, interact in person or in the same digital space | High | High |

Design 3-5 touches per first-10 entry before any ask is made. The ask happens only
after the relationship has real substance — they know who wzrdx is and have already
received value.

**Warm-up tracking:**

| Name | Touch 1 (type + date) | Touch 2 | Touch 3 | Touch 4 | Touch 5 | Ready for ask? |
|------|-----------------------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------------|
| ... | ... | | | | | No / Yes |

The "ready for ask" judgment: they have acknowledged or responded to at least two
touches, and the relationship feels genuinely mutual, not transactional.

### 5. Parallel outreach for whales (Martell protocol)

For Category C (whales), the Martell parallel-outreach principle applies: never build
a single whale relationship as if it is the company's only shot. Run 5-10 whale
relationships in parallel so that:
- No single "no" collapses the pipeline.
- The scarcity is real (you have other options).
- The momentum is self-reinforcing (whales talk to each other).

Parallel outreach execution:

1. **Select 5-10 whale candidates** from Category C (not 1, not 20 — 5-10 is the
   operational limit without losing depth).
2. **Assign each a dedicated warm-up sequence** (§4). Do not use the same sequence
   for all — whales notice when outreach is templated.
3. **Run all 5-10 simultaneously.** Every week, advance at least one touch per whale.
4. **Track momentum, not just status.** A whale that has responded twice and shared
   your content is further along than one that only opened an email. Use the warm-up
   tracker (§4) as the momentum gauge.
5. **First ask — partnership offer structure** (§6). Make the ask only when the
   relationship is warm AND you have a specific, mutual-win proposal ready.

Never put a whale in the same cadence as a cold email prospect. Whales require the
sniper-lane personalisation from `growth:cold-outreach-engine` (Loom + 5-min research
pass) for any cold first touch — but the Dream 100 frame means warm-up comes before
that touch.

### 6. Partnership offer structure

When the relationship is warm and the ask is ready, present a specific, mutual-win
partnership proposal. Structure:

```
Partnership Offer — [wzrdx] × [Partner name] — [YYYY-MM-DD]

What we are building together
[1-2 sentences: the specific partnership model and why it makes sense for both sides.]

What you get
- [Specific benefit 1 for the partner — revenue share, referral fee, exclusive access,
  co-branding, content collaboration, introductions.]
- [Specific benefit 2]
- [Specific benefit 3]

What we get
- [Honest statement of wzrdx's goal — introductions to their clients, distribution
  to their audience, co-marketing, anchor case study.]

How it works
[Step-by-step: how referrals flow, how revenue is shared, what each side does.]

Pilot proposal
[Start small — a 90-day pilot with a defined success metric before a longer commitment.
Reduces the partner's risk and creates a natural checkpoint.]

Next step
[Single action: "A 30-minute call to align on the details — [booking link]"]
```

Route the partnership offer to the CMO for messaging review and to the CFO + CEO
for any revenue-share or pricing commitment before sending.

### 7. Dream 100 operating rhythm

The Dream 100 is a system, not a one-time exercise. It requires a weekly operating
rhythm:

**Weekly (15 minutes):**
- Advance at least one touch for each of the first-10 entries.
- Move any entry from "warm-up in progress" to "ready for ask" if the relationship
  has reached the threshold.
- Add any new entry that appeared on radar (event, content, referral).

**Monthly (30 minutes):**
- Review the full 100-entry list — who has moved to "partner" (active relationship)?
  Who has gone cold and needs a new warm-up approach?
- Add 10 new entries to replace those who converted or became inactive.
- Report Dream 100 pipeline progress to the CRO weekly review.

**Quarterly:**
- Full audit of the Dream 100 list — remove entries that will never convert, add a
  new category tier if a new ICP segment has been validated.
- Review which category (A/B/C) produced the most revenue and adjust the list weight
  accordingly.

After the initial build and after each monthly review, `kb_ingest`:

- Tags: `dream-100`, `partnerships`, `growth`, `whales`, `referral`, `<company-slug>`.
- Include: the full master list (100 entries), first-10 selection and rationale,

Monthly review:
- Title: `Dream 100 Review — [Company] — [YYYY-MM]`.
- Tags: `dream-100`, `partnerships`, `growth`, `monthly-review`.
- Include: status updates per entry, new entries added, revenue attributed to Dream 100
  relationships, next month's priority touches.

## Output contract

Every Dream 100 session produces:
1. **Master list** — entries across all three categories with name, why, relationship
   status, and warm-up status (§2).
2. **First-10 plan** — selected entries with prioritisation rationale (§3).
3. **Warm-up sequences** — 3-5 value-first touches per first-10 entry (§4).
4. **Whale parallel outreach plan** — 5-10 whale candidates with dedicated sequences
   (§5).
5. **Partnership offer template** — the mutual-win structure, ready to personalise (§6).
6. **Weekly operating rhythm** — 15-min weekly + 30-min monthly review protocol (§7).
7. **Next action** — single most important Dream 100 action this week. Owner, deadline.

## Red flags

- Asking too early — a partnership request after one value touch is not a Dream 100
  relationship; it is cold outreach in disguise and will be treated as such.
- Building the list without the mutual-win filter — a "dream" partner who gains
  nothing from the relationship is not a partner; they are a prospect. Every entry
  must have a clear answer to "what do they get?"
- Running a single whale relationship as the company's primary growth bet — Martell's
  parallel protocol exists for this reason; one whale ghosting you must not stall the
  growth engine.
- Using templated warm-up for all 100 entries — the value-first touch must be
  genuinely specific to the recipient; a generic "loved your content" comment is
  noise, not relationship building.
- Neglecting the operating rhythm — a Dream 100 list that is not touched weekly goes
  stale; relationships cool faster than pipelines.
- Routing partnership offers with revenue-share commitments to only CMO — any pricing
  or revenue split requires CFO + CEO gate before the offer goes out.
- Not ingesting the tracker — a Dream 100 that lives only in a spreadsheet cannot
  survive a context switch or a team handover; it must be in the KB.
