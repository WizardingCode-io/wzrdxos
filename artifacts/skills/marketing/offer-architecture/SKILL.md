# Offer Architecture

Flexible capability skill. Takes a product, service or campaign and produces the
messaging layer of the offer — the copy structure and value framing that makes it
irresistible — grounded in the Obsidian KB frameworks.

**Scope boundary:** this skill owns messaging, copy and structure. Pricing levels,
unit economics, payback period and tier anchoring belong to `fin:pricing-redesign`
(CFO + CEO gate). Both skills should run in sequence for a complete offer launch:
`marketing:offer-architecture` first (messaging), then `fin:pricing-redesign`
(pricing validation).

Anchored on: Alex Hormozi (Grand Slam Offer — dream outcome, perceived likelihood,
time delay, effort; value stack; risk reversal); Sabri Suby (17-step sales message
structure); Russell Brunson (value ladder, lead magnet positioning); Neil Patel
(landing-page structure and conversion). All sourced from the WizardingCode vault
(Topics — Offer Design & Pricing, Grand Slam Offer framework; Personas — Hormozi
169 sources, Suby 775 lines, Brunson 799 lines, Patel).

## Steps

### 1. KB-first

`kb_search` and `kb_ask` for:
- Company profile (`wzrdx company`) — offer set, ICP, positioning, pricing tier.
- Existing offers, landing pages, proposals or copy already in the KB.
- Grand Slam Offer framework (Hormozi) — the four value levers.
- 17-step Sabri Suby sales message structure from the Sales Frameworks topic.
- NUEPH framework — Novo, Único, Exclusivo, Para quem, Hostil — for positioning the
  offer against the status quo and competitors.
- Prior win/loss notes or deal debriefs — what objections and desires surfaced.
- Rebranding-2026-06 verbal identity (once locked) — copy must align with brand voice.

Surface what already exists. Do not rewrite offers that are already documented and
performing unless the task is a deliberate refresh.

### 2. Define the offer context

Before writing anything, establish the five anchors:

1. **Product / service being offered** — what exactly is being sold? State the
   deliverable, the timeline and the specific outcome in concrete terms.
2. **ICP** — who is the target buyer? Be specific: industry, role, company size,
   revenue stage, trigger event that makes them ready to buy now.
3. **Single biggest pain** — what is the one problem the ICP urgently needs solved?
   (The offer must answer this problem, not describe the product.)
4. **Dream outcome** — what does the ICP's life look like after the problem is solved?
   State it in their words, not the company's.
5. **Competing alternatives** — what would the ICP do instead of buying? (Status quo,
   competitor, DIY.) The offer must be clearly superior to each alternative.

If any anchor is missing, ask for it before proceeding. An offer without a defined
ICP and dream outcome cannot be Grand Slam — it is a product description.

### 3. Build the Grand Slam Offer — four value levers

Apply Hormozi's four levers. The perceived value of the offer is:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Success)
        ÷ (Time Delay × Effort and Sacrifice)
```

For each lever, document the specific claim and the evidence or mechanism that
supports it:

**Lever 1 — Dream Outcome (maximise)**
- State the dream outcome explicitly: "You will [specific result] by [specific date]."
- Make it concrete, measurable and emotionally resonant. Avoid vague aspirations.
- The dream outcome is what the ICP actually wants, not what the product does.
  Example: not "AI-powered workflow automation" but "your team completes onboarding
  in 3 days instead of 3 weeks, and your founder is no longer the bottleneck."

**Lever 2 — Perceived Likelihood of Success (maximise)**
- What evidence makes the outcome credible? Choose from: case studies (name vertical
  and result), testimonials (quote + outcome + time), data (specific metric),
  credentials (relevant certification, years, client count), mechanism (the proprietary
  process that produces the result), or risk reversal (the guarantee — see §5).
- Produce the specific credibility block for this offer. If case studies do not yet
  exist, flag this to CRO (production of the first 3-5 real case studies is a known
  gap from the Diagnostico).

**Lever 3 — Time Delay (minimise)**
- How fast does the ICP feel the first result? State the first win timeline, not just
  the full outcome timeline.
- Structure the offer to front-load the early win: "Within the first week, you will
  see X." The sooner the ICP experiences value, the lower the perceived risk.

**Lever 4 — Effort and Sacrifice (minimise)**
- What does the ICP NOT have to do because of this offer? "Done-with-you", "we handle
  everything", "no technical knowledge required" — the minimal-effort framing is a
  value amplifier.
- Name the specific burdens the offer removes. Each burden removed is a reason to buy.

### 4. Value stack — unbundle and restackate

A single-line offer feels expensive. A stacked offer feels like a deal.

Unbundle every component of the offer and assign a standalone perceived value to each:

| Component | What it is | Perceived value (€) | Note |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|------|
| Core deliverable | ... | ... | The primary result |
| Bonus 1 | ... | ... | Something they would pay for separately |
| Bonus 2 | ... | ... | ... |
| Fast-action incentive | ... | ... | Scarcity or urgency element |
| Guarantee | ... | ... | The risk reversal (see §5) |
| **Total stack value** | | **€X** | |
| **Actual investment** | | **€Y** | Y << X |

Rules for the value stack:
- Every component must deliver real, standalone value — no inflated phantom bonuses.
- The total stack value must be at minimum 3× the actual investment (Hormozi: make
  the offer feel like stealing).
- The fast-action incentive must be genuinely scarce or time-limited — do not fake it.
- Align the final investment figure with `fin:pricing-redesign` before publishing.

### 5. Risk reversal — design the guarantee

The risk reversal is the single highest-leverage copy element. It transfers the
downside from the buyer to the company, making the decision nearly risk-free.

Choose the strongest guarantee the company can credibly stand behind:

| Type | Structure | When to use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Results guarantee | "If you don't achieve [X] by [date], we [refund / redo / extend]." | When results are measurable and consistently achievable |
| Effort guarantee | "If you do [Y steps] and don't see [Z], we refund." | When results depend partly on the buyer's actions |
| Pilot / trial | "Start with [scope] — if not happy after [period], you owe nothing." | High-ticket or high-trust-required offers |
| Satisfaction guarantee | "Not satisfied? Tell us why. We fix it or refund." | Broad guarantee; weakest — use only if no results guarantee is feasible |

Write the guarantee in plain language — one sentence, no conditions buried in
fine print. The guarantee must be prominent in the copy (not hidden in terms).

Cross-reference with CFO + CEO before committing to a results guarantee — the company
must be able to absorb the financial downside if it triggers.

### 6. 17-step Sabri Suby sales message

Structure the landing page, email sequence or proposal copy following the 17-step
framework (adapted from the WizardingCode KB — Sales Frameworks topic, Suby persona):

```
1.  Hook / Pattern interrupt
    [The first sentence stops the scroll — a question, a bold claim, or a stated pain.
    See marketing:hook-viral-system for the 7 hook types.]

2.  Define the problem (in the ICP's words)
    [Name the pain state. "If you're like most [ICP role], you're dealing with [pain]."]

3.  Amplify the pain
    [Make the problem feel urgent. What happens if it stays unsolved? What does it
    cost — in time, money, opportunity, peace of mind?]

4.  Introduce the solution category
    [Name the approach — not the product yet. "The solution is [mechanism]."]

5.  State your credibility
    [Why you? Case studies, results, years, clients. The credibility block from §3.]

6.  State the dream outcome
    [The exact before→after the ICP achieves. Their life after the problem is solved.]

7.  Social proof (case study or testimonial)
    [One specific case study: who they were, what they did, what result they got and
    when. Specific beats generic — "€40K revenue in 60 days" beats "great results".]

8.  Bullets of benefit
    [5-7 bullets of the specific things the ICP gets. Lead with the outcome, not the
    feature. Format: "You get [outcome] without [sacrifice]."]

9.  Reveal the offer / value stack
    [Present the full stacked offer from §4. Name each component + its standalone value.]

10. Price anchor (handled by fin:pricing-redesign)
    [The price anchor — what comparable solutions charge — is set in coordination with
    CFO. Placeholder here: "Typically [anchored alternative] costs €X. Your investment
    today is €Y."]

11. Risk reversal
    [Present the guarantee from §5 in full. Make it impossible to miss.]

12. Urgency / scarcity
    [Why act now? Real urgency only — spots, cohort dates, bonus expiry. Do not fake it.]

13. Call to action (primary)
    [One action. One link. "Click here to [specific next step]."]

14. P.S. — summary re-statement
    [Readers scroll to the bottom. The P.S. summarises: problem, offer, outcome, CTA.]

15. FAQ — objection pre-emption
    [5-7 questions the ICP's inner skeptic asks. Answer each directly. No spin.]

16. Second call to action
    [Repeat the primary CTA after the FAQ for readers who were convinced by the answers.]

17. Closing re-anchor
    [One final sentence connecting back to the dream outcome. "This is your chance
    to [dream outcome]. [CTA]."]
```

Produce the populated scaffold for the current offer — fill every step with
specific, ICP-appropriate copy. Leave no step as a placeholder in the output.

### 7. Review against NUEPH

Run the completed message through the NUEPH test to confirm positioning:

- **N — Novo:** does the messaging signal something new? A mechanism, an approach, or
  an era the ICP has not seen before?
- **U — Único:** is the offer clearly differentiated from the next best alternative?
  What is the one thing wzrdx is #1 at (Hormozi #1-At-What)?
- **E — Exclusivo:** does the framing imply that not everyone can access this? (Elite
  clients, application process, limited spots, expertise barrier.)
- **P — Para quem:** is the ICP stated precisely? "This is for you if..." and "This is
  NOT for you if..." (exclusion increases desire among the right buyers).
- **H — Hostil:** does the copy take a position? A memorable offer is mildly hostile to
  the status quo or to the ICP's current approach — it calls out a lie or a false
  belief the market holds.

Score: N/U/E/P/H each green (Y) or red (N). Revise copy for any red score.

## Output contract

Every offer architecture session produces:
1. **Offer context** — ICP, dream outcome, pain, and competing alternatives (§2).
2. **Four-lever analysis** — each lever with the specific claim and its evidence (§3).
3. **Value stack table** — all components with perceived values and total (§4).
4. **Guarantee** — the chosen risk reversal, written in plain language (§5).
5. **17-step sales message scaffold** — fully populated for the offer (§6).
6. **NUEPH scorecard** — N/U/E/P/H green/red with gap notes (§7).
7. **Next action** — single most important copy improvement now. Owner, deadline.

## Red flags

- Writing copy before defining the ICP and dream outcome — copy without a specific
  buyer in mind is a product description, not a sales message.
- Setting price within this skill — pricing levels require CFO + CEO gate; cross-ref
  `fin:pricing-redesign` and never publish a price without that approval.
- A value stack where the total is less than 3× the investment — the offer must feel
  like undeniable value; if it does not, add components or reduce the price (CFO gate).
- A risk reversal the company cannot actually honour — a guarantee you cannot absorb
  triggers reputational and financial damage; validate with CFO before committing.
- Faking urgency or scarcity — manufactured pressure erodes trust when the prospect
  notices; only use real constraints.
- NUEPH scores with reds unfixed — a red on Único means the offer is commoditised;
  a red on Para quem means the copy speaks to everyone and converts no one.
- Skipping the KB ingest — offer copy that is not documented is recreated from scratch
  on every iteration, compounding no learnings.
