# Hook & Viral System

Flexible capability skill. Takes a content piece, a campaign idea, or a raw topic
and produces a full hook set plus a STEPPS virality scorecard. Applies the first-3-
seconds discipline across all formats — video opens, email subjects, post openers,
ad headlines and thumbnail copy.

Anchored on: the Hook Architecture framework (7 hook types — from the WizardingCode
vault, KB Area 16 Conteúdo & Viralização); Jonah Berger's STEPPS framework (Social
Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories — from the vault,
Marketing & Growth topic); Sabri Suby (pattern interrupt, curiosity engineering);
Alex Hormozi (volume approach to hooks — test many, keep what converts); Russell
Brunson (Attractive Character, story-based hooks).

## Steps

### 1. KB-first

`kb_search` and `kb_ask` for:
- Company profile (`wzrdx company`) — brand voice, ICP, content pillars.
- Existing content pieces, hooks, or subject lines already tested and documented.
- Hook Architecture framework from the KB — 7 types, first-3-seconds rules.
- STEPPS framework — all six levers with application patterns.
- Rebranding-2026-06 verbal identity — hooks must match the brand voice and
  positioning direction (once locked).
- Content calendar — is this hook for a scheduled piece or an ad-hoc idea?

Surface what has been tested. Build on what worked; do not repeat what did not.

### 2. Define the content context

Before generating anything, establish:

1. **Format** — video (Reel/Short/YouTube), post (LinkedIn/Instagram), email, ad,
   thumbnail, podcast title, or newsletter subject. Format determines hook length and
   structure.
2. **Topic** — the single idea the content delivers. If the topic is vague, narrow it:
   one piece, one claim, one audience.
3. **ICP state** — what is the target audience thinking and feeling when they encounter
   this content? What are they scrolling past? What pattern needs to be interrupted?
4. **Content goal** — stop the scroll only (awareness), drive a click (traffic), drive
   a reply or share (engagement), or drive a CTA action (conversion)?
5. **Platform norms** — what hooks perform on this platform right now? (LinkedIn
   favours curiosity gaps and bold opinions; Instagram/Reels favour fast visual hooks
   and relatable pain; email subjects favour specificity and personalisation.)

A hook generated without knowing the format and platform is a guess. Define both first.

### 3. Apply the first-3-seconds discipline

The first 3 seconds (or first sentence for text) determine whether the audience
continues. Apply the discipline across formats:

**Video (Reels / YouTube Shorts / TikTok):**
- Frame 0: the hook is visible before audio. Text overlay + visual must communicate
  the promise in silence. Assume muted playback.
- First spoken word: start mid-action or mid-sentence. Never start with "Hey guys",
  "Welcome back", or "Today I'm going to". Start with the payoff or the question.
- First 3 seconds must answer: "Why should I keep watching?" — curiosity, shock,
  relevance, or an unresolved tension.

**Email subject lines:**
- Max 7 words for mobile preview. The first 3 words must contain the hook.
- No "Newsletter #47" or "Update from [Company]". Lead with the promise, the pain,
  or the curiosity trigger.
- Test a curiosity version (open loop) against a specificity version (concrete result):
  both from the 7 hook types (§4).

**LinkedIn / social posts:**
- First line visible before "see more" is the only line that matters initially.
- It must either: state a bold claim, call out a specific pain, open a curiosity gap,
  or start a story with an unresolved situation.
- Sentence length ≤ 12 words. No "I've been thinking about..." openers.

**Thumbnails:**
- 3 elements: face (emotion), text (the promise in 3-5 words), visual contrast.
  Each element must work independently of the others.
- Text must be readable at 100px. Remove any word that is not load-bearing.

### 4. Generate the hook set — 7 types

Produce at minimum one hook per type for the content topic. Label each with its type.

**Type 1 — Curiosity gap:**
State enough to promise a payoff but withhold enough to create a gap the audience must
close. "The one mistake that kills most [ICP role]'s [desired outcome]." The gap must
be credible — do not overpromise.

**Type 2 — Bold claim / contrarian:**
State something the ICP believes is wrong or has never heard stated so directly.
"[Conventional wisdom] is the reason you're not getting [desired result]." High
attention, high controversy — back it with evidence in the body.

**Type 3 — Pain agitation:**
Name the exact pain the ICP is feeling right now. "If you're still doing [bad habit],
you're leaving [specific loss] on the table every month." Mirror the internal monologue.

**Type 4 — Specific result (social proof hook):**
Lead with a specific, credible result. "[Number] in [timeframe] from [starting point]."
Specificity is credibility. Vague results are unbelievable; specific results are magnetic.

**Type 5 — Story hook (open loop):**
Start mid-story with an unresolved situation. "Three months ago, [ICP situation].
Today, [outcome]. Here's what changed." The audience must know the ending.

**Type 6 — Question (the Socratic hook):**
Ask the one question the ICP is already asking themselves. "Are you still doing
[status-quo behaviour] in [year]?" or "What would change if you [dream outcome]?"
The question must be genuinely interesting — not rhetorical filler.

**Type 7 — Instruction / how-to (authority hook):**
Lead with a direct command or a numbered promise. "Do [specific action] before [event]
and you'll [outcome]." or "3 things [top performers] do that [average] don't."
Authority hooks work when the ICP has already decided they want to learn; use them
for search-intent content or email nurture sequences.

**Output format:**

| Type | Hook text | Format fit | Estimated attention score (1-5) |
|------|-----------|------------|--------------------------------|
| Curiosity gap | ... | Video / LinkedIn | 4 |
| Bold claim | ... | ... | ... |
| ... | | | |

Generate at minimum 7 hooks (one per type). For high-priority content (launch,
campaign, paid ad), generate 3-5 variants per type and flag the strongest 3 for A/B
testing.

### 5. STEPPS virality scorecard

For a piece of content or a campaign, score it against all six STEPPS levers. A high
STEPPS score predicts organic sharing and word-of-mouth amplification.

**S — Social Currency:**
Does sharing this content make the sharer look good — knowledgeable, ahead of the
curve, in-the-know, or part of an exclusive group? If sharing it reflects positively
on the sharer, it spreads. Score: 1-5.

**T — Triggers:**
Is there an environmental cue that will remind the audience of this content repeatedly?
The best triggers are high-frequency and relevant (Monday, morning, every time they
open [tool], every time they see [event]). "Monday = [content topic]" is a trigger.
Score: 1-5.

**E — Emotion:**
Does the content evoke a high-arousal emotion — awe, anxiety, anger, admiration, or
amusement? Low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment) do not drive sharing. Identify
the target emotion explicitly. Score: 1-5.

**P — Public:**
Is the behaviour of engaging with or sharing this content observable by others? Content
that is visible in public feeds scores higher than DM-only content. Design the content
to have a public signal — a shareable quote, a repost hook, a "tag someone who needs
this" CTA. Score: 1-5.

**P — Practical Value:**
Is this content immediately useful? "News you can use" — actionable, specific,
applicable today. A tactical checklist scores higher than an inspirational thought.
Score: 1-5.

**S — Stories:**
Is there a narrative wrapper? Stories carry information more memorably than lists.
If the content is a list of tips, is there a before/after story or a case study
framing that makes it stick? Score: 1-5.

**Virality scorecard output:**

| Lever | Score (1-5) | Gap / opportunity |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Social Currency | | |
| Triggers | | |
| Emotion | | |
| Public | | |
| Practical Value | | |
| Stories | | |
| **Total** | **/30** | |

Interpretation:
- 24-30: high virality potential — publish, distribute widely, boost if budget allows.
- 15-23: moderate — strengthen the lowest-scoring lever before publishing.
- < 15: low — redesign the content concept before investing in production.

Produce one specific improvement action for each lever scoring below 3.

### 6. Select and finalise the hook

From the generated hook set (§4) and the STEPPS audit (§5):

1. Select the hook that best matches the format, the STEPPS gaps, and the brand voice.
2. Write the first-3-seconds execution: the exact text, visual direction (for video),
   or subject line phrasing.
3. Identify the A/B test variant — pick a second hook of a different type to test
   against the primary.
4. State the success metric for the format: view-through rate (video), open rate
   (email), click rate (post), or CTR (ad).

### 7. Ingest hook learnings to KB

After a hook goes live and performance data is available, `kb_ingest`:
- Title: `Hook Test — [Content topic] — [Format] — [YYYY-MM-DD]`.
- Tags: `hook`, `viral`, `stepps`, `content`, `marketing`, `<format>`, `<topic-slug>`.
- Include: the hook set generated, the STEPPS scorecard, the winning hook selected,
  the performance metric result, and what hook type to prioritise next for this format.

Hook performance data is the most valuable marketing learning asset — document every test.

## Output contract

Every hook and viral session produces:
1. **Content context** — format, topic, ICP state, goal, platform (§2).
2. **Hook set** — at minimum 7 hooks (one per type) with format fit and attention score
   (§4).
3. **STEPPS scorecard** — six-lever score (out of 30) with improvement actions for
   levers below 3 (§5).
4. **Selected hook + A/B variant** — the primary hook with first-3-seconds execution
   notes and the test variant (§6).
5. **Success metric** — what to measure and by what date to evaluate performance.
6. **KB ingest plan** — title and tags for when performance data is available.

## Red flags

- Generating hooks without defining the format first — a hook for a LinkedIn post and
  a hook for a YouTube Reel are different instruments; format defines length, structure
  and first-frame discipline.
- Hooks that open with the brand name or product name — the first word must earn
  attention, not assume it; the brand is revealed after the hook lands.
- A STEPPS score below 15 with no redesign plan — low-virality content is a sunk cost
  waiting to happen; invest in the redesign before investing in production.
- A hook set with all 7 from the same type — variety is the point; each type reaches
  a different audience segment and emotional state.
- Testing hooks without recording results in the KB — hook intelligence compounds only
  when performance data is ingested and retrievable for future campaigns.
- Skipping the first-3-seconds check for video — a hook that works in text does not
  automatically work in video; apply the silent-muted test to every video hook.
