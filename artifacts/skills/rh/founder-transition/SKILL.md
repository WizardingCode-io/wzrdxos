# Founder Transition

Flexible capability skill. Guides the founder through the psychological transition
from operator (doing everything) to leader (building the system that does everything).
Identifies the specific blocker with evidence, applies structured reframes, proposes
a mentoring cadence, and hands off to the practical delegation and buyback tools.

Anchored on the Diagnostico-2026-05 founder-blocker protocol (perfectionism, fear
of losing control, indispensability — diagnosed as the root constraint at
WizardingCode), Dan Martell's "80% by others > 100% alone" reframe, and the
Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test.

## Steps

### 1. KB-first

`kb_search` and `kb_ask` for:
- Company profile (`wzrdx company`) — current team, revenue stage, what the founder
  is currently doing day-to-day.
- Diagnostico-2026-05 — the root constraint diagnosis (founder centralisation,
  Estructura 28.4/100, perfectionism and control patterns, 5 failed hires, D1-D180
  roadmap milestones).
- Prior founder-transition coaching notes or delegation postmortems in the KB.
- Dan Martell's Buyback Rate and 92% Rule — for the practical handoff after
  the blocker is named.
- No-How-To Delegation and Standards-Not-Presence frameworks.
- Teach-Don't-Do identity reframe.

Surface the Diagnostico founder-blocker evidence before starting — the coaching
must be grounded in the real pattern, not a generic protocol.

### 2. Blocker identification — name the pattern with evidence

The three founder-transition blockers (from the Diagnostico protocol):

**Blocker A — Perfectionism:**
- The founder believes that only their work meets the required standard.
- Evidence signals: reworks others' output > 30% of the time; says "it is faster
  to do it myself"; has never approved a delegated output without editing it;
  clients complain when the founder is not the direct deliverer.

**Blocker B — Fear of losing control:**
- The founder does not trust that work will get done correctly without their
  oversight.
- Evidence signals: checks in on delegated tasks daily (unsolicited); defines
  the method, not just the outcome; cancels delegation under deadline pressure;
  asks for progress updates before the agreed check-in.

**Blocker C — Indispensability:**
- The founder believes that the company needs them to function; their identity
  is tied to being the necessary person.
- Evidence signals: has not taken ≥ 5 consecutive work days off in > 12 months;
  client calls come directly to the founder's mobile; team does not know the
  answers to questions the founder has never documented; founder is the only
  person who can approve decisions above €200.

Run the blocker identification interview:

1. "When you think about someone else handling [task], what is the first thought
   that comes up?"
2. "In the last 30 days, how many times did you step back into a task you had
   delegated?"
3. "If you were unavailable for two weeks, what would break first?"
4. "What is the last thing you delegated that you were fully satisfied with?"
5. "When a team member does something 80% as well as you would — what do you do?"

From the answers, name the primary blocker (A, B, or C) and provide the three
evidence sentences that confirm it. If two blockers co-exist, name both and note
which is primary (the one that will be addressed first).

**State the consequence of the blocker remaining unaddressed for the next 90 days**
(link to the Diagnostico D1-D180 roadmap milestone — e.g. "if this is not resolved
by D60, the first autonomous hire target of D90 cannot succeed").

### 3. The reframe — 80% by others > 100% alone

Dan Martell's core reframe: **it is better to have something done at 80% of your
quality by someone else, consistently and without your time, than to do it at 100%
quality yourself, every time, consuming your finite hours.**

This is not a compromise — it is a leverage decision.

Apply the reframe to the specific blocker:

**For Perfectionism (Blocker A):**
- "The standard you are protecting is real. The question is not 'does this person
  meet my standard?' — the question is 'can I define that standard clearly enough
  that someone else can meet it without me in the loop?' If you cannot define the
  standard in writing, the problem is not the delegate — it is the standard."
- Transition action: run `rh:delegation-coach` for the top 3 tasks where
  perfectionism is blocking delegation. The delegation card's quality-standard
  section is the test: if the standard can be written, it can be delegated.

**For Fear of losing control (Blocker B):**
- "Control over outcomes is legitimate. Control over methods is micromanagement.
  The moment you tell someone how to do their job instead of what done looks like,
  you have taken back the delegation. You cannot delegate and control at the same
  time — you have to choose which one you trust."
- Transition action: implement Standards-Not-Presence for all current delegations
  (from `rh:delegation-coach`). Remove all unscheduled check-ins; schedule only
  the agreed cadence.

**For Indispensability (Blocker C):**
- "Being indispensable today is not a compliment — it is a constraint. Every day
  you are the only person who can make a decision or answer a question, you are
  capping the company's growth at the ceiling of your own hours. The company you
  want to build cannot be built by one person."
- Transition action: run `admin:buyback-audit` to quantify the hours being consumed
  by below-threshold tasks. The buyback audit is the evidence that the founder's
  hours are finite and being spent on the wrong things.

### 4. Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test

The Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test is a structured self-assessment:

**For each of the following statements, rate 1 (never) to 5 (always):**

| Statement | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| I feel more comfortable doing the work than reviewing someone else's work. | |
| I feel uncomfortable when I cannot answer a client question directly. | |
| When something goes wrong, my first instinct is to take it back from the person handling it. | |
| I am the one who knows where everything is / how everything works. | |
| I feel most productive when I am in execution mode, not when I am planning or coaching. | |
| My self-worth is linked to how much I am personally contributing to outputs. | |

**Scoring:**
- 6-12: Low insecurity — you are functionally ready to lead; the transition is
  primarily a systems and habits problem (route to `rh:delegation-coach` and
  `admin:buyback-audit`).
- 13-20: Moderate insecurity — the identity work is needed alongside the systems.
  Run both the reframe (this skill) and the practical tools in parallel.
- 21-30: High insecurity — the identity work is the prerequisite; systems will
  not hold until the identity shift is underway. Start here; layer the practical
  tools in week 3.

State the test result and the sequencing implication explicitly.

### 5. Mentoring cadence proposal

The transition requires structured, ongoing coaching — not a one-time intervention.

Propose a mentoring cadence calibrated to the blocker type and test score:

**For Low insecurity (score 6-12) — systems cadence:**
- Weekly: 30-minute self-review of the decision journal (see step 6).
- Bi-weekly: 45-minute coaching call with an external advisor or peer founder —
  review one delegated area, name what was let go and what the result was.
- Monthly: full review of the `admin:buyback-audit` output — is the Buyback Rate
  improving?

**For Moderate insecurity (score 13-20) — hybrid cadence:**
- Weekly: 30-minute reflection + decision journal.
- Weekly (also): one structured conversation with a team member using the
  coaching questions from step 3 — not task management, but a genuine coaching
  conversation.
- Monthly: external advisor or peer founder session.
- Quarterly: re-run this skill to reassess blocker and test score.

**For High insecurity (score 21-30) — intensive identity cadence:**
- Daily (first 30 days): morning journaling prompt: "What am I going to let someone
  else do today that I would normally do myself? What is the belief that makes
  that feel uncomfortable?"
- Weekly: coaching session with external advisor (business coach, peer founder,
  or mentor who has made this transition).
- Weekly: share one thing from the decision journal with a trusted team member
  (accountability).
- Monthly: re-run the Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test to track movement.
- Re-run this skill at day 30 and day 60.

### 6. Decision journal — the accountability tool

The decision journal is the practical accountability artefact for the transition:

Each week, the founder records:
1. **One thing I let go this week:** [task, decision, or responsibility delegated
   or not picked back up after being delegated].
2. **What the result was:** [outcome — did the standard get met? was the delegate
   autonomous?]
3. **One thing I did not let go (and should have):** [named honestly].
4. **The belief that stopped me:** [from the blocker identification — named
   explicitly].
5. **What I will do differently next week:** [one specific behavioural change].

The journal is private unless the founder chooses to share it with an advisor.
It is the primary data source for the bi-weekly or monthly mentor review.

### 7. Handoff to practical tools

After the blocker is named and the reframe is applied, route to the practical
arm:

- **`rh:delegation-coach`:** for each task the founder needs to delegate next.
  Run one delegation card per week for the first month.
- **`admin:buyback-audit`:** to quantify total hours in below-threshold tasks and
  build the delegation priority list.
- **`rh:comp-ladder`:** if indispensability is linked to being the only person
  who knows how to do senior-level work — closing the compensation gap may be
  prerequisite to finding a qualified delegate.

State the sequence explicitly:
1. This skill (blocker + reframe + cadence) — week 1.
2. `admin:buyback-audit` — week 2.
3. First `rh:delegation-coach` session — week 3.
4. Repeat at weekly cadence for 90 days.

### 8. Transition plan with weekly checkpoints

Produce the 90-day transition plan:

**Week 1-2 — Diagnostic and reframe:**
- Complete this skill.
- Run `admin:buyback-audit`.
- Name the first 3 tasks to delegate.

**Week 3-4 — First delegations:**
- Run `rh:delegation-coach` for each of the 3 tasks.
- Implement the Standards-Not-Presence cadence.
- Start the decision journal.

**Week 5-8 — Consolidation:**
- Weekly decision journal review.
- First mentor/advisor session.
- First 30-day review of delegated tasks (standard met? blockers surfaced?).
- Identify 3 more tasks to delegate.

**Week 9-12 — Autonomy build:**
- Re-run Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test — has the score moved?
- 90-day review of all delegated tasks.
- Define what "the founder's 8%" looks like at month 12.
- Re-run `admin:buyback-audit` — compare to week-2 baseline.
- Route findings to KB.

**Weekly checkpoint template (for the mentor/advisor session):**
1. What did I let go this week?
2. What was the result?
3. What did I pull back (and why)?
4. What is the one thing I will protect from my 8% this week?

### 9. Ingest to KB

After producing the transition plan, ingest via `kb_ingest`:
- Title: `Founder Transition Plan — <Company> — <YYYY-MM>`.
- Source path: `memos/rh/founder-transition-<company-slug>-<YYYY-MM>`.
- Tags: `transição`, `rh`, `fundador`, `delegação`, `bloqueadores`,
  `<company-slug>`.
- Include: blocker identification with evidence, test score and interpretation,
  reframe notes, mentoring cadence, 90-day plan with weekly checkpoints.

At day 30 and day 90: ingest a progress memo updating the blocker status and
test score.

## Output contract

The deliverable always contains:
1. **Blocker identification** — primary blocker (A/B/C) with three evidence
   sentences and consequence of inaction in 90 days.
2. **80% reframe** — blocker-specific reframe with transition action.
3. **Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test** — scored table, result, sequencing
   implication.
4. **Mentoring cadence** — calibrated to test score, with specific frequency
   and format.
5. **Decision journal template** — 5-question weekly artefact.
6. **Handoff sequence** — explicit routing to `rh:delegation-coach` and
   `admin:buyback-audit` in sequenced order.
7. **90-day transition plan** — week-by-week with named milestones.
8. **Weekly checkpoint template** — 4-question format for mentor/advisor sessions.
9. **Next action** — single most important transition action this week, owner
   (the founder), deadline.

## Red flags

- Running the reframe before naming the blocker with evidence — a generic "let go
  more" message is not coaching; a named pattern with evidence is.
- Skipping the Leader vs Manager Insecurity Test — the test result determines the
  sequencing; without it, the intervention may be too light or too heavy.
- Sending the founder straight to `rh:delegation-coach` without the identity
  work for high-insecurity cases — the practical tool will fail if the identity
  blocker is not named first.
- Defining "the founder's 8%" before running `admin:buyback-audit` — the audit
  reveals the real time allocation; the 8% cannot be defined from intuition.
- Producing a 90-day plan without weekly checkpoints — a monthly review is too
  infrequent to catch the pull-back behaviour before it embeds.
- Not routing to `rh:comp-ladder` when indispensability is linked to being the
  only qualified person — if the reason there is no delegate is that no one can
  be hired for the role at the current salary, the comp problem is the root cause.
- Not ingesting the plan — the transition history must be in the KB so day-30
  and day-90 progress memos can compare against the original diagnosis.
